# The Palme assassination: a complete factual dossier

**Olof Palme, Sweden's Prime Minister, was shot dead at 23:21 on February 28, 1986, while walking home from a cinema with his wife on a snowy Stockholm street — and 40 years later, the case remains officially unsolved.** The murder triggered the largest criminal investigation in Swedish history: **700,000 pages** of documents, **10,000+ people** interviewed, **134 false confessions**, a cost exceeding **SEK 600 million (~$71 million)**, and a succession of eight chief prosecutors across 34 years. In 2020, prosecutor Krister Petersson named graphic designer Stig Engström as the probable killer and closed the case — but in December 2025, that designation was reversed, leaving the murder without any official suspect. What follows is a granular factual reconstruction across every dimension of the case.

## 1. The day: February 28, 1986, minute by minute

### Morning and workday

**~09:00** — Palme plays tennis at **Kungliga Tennishallen** (Royal Tennis Hall) in Östermalm with longtime friend **Harry Schein**. Both men are 59. Palme wins narrowly.

**After tennis** — His two SÄPO bodyguards drive him to **Ströms herrekipering** (men's clothing store) on Sveavägen, where he returns or adjusts a suit ordered for an upcoming Moscow trip in April. The bodyguards are irritated — they didn't know he had visited the store alone previously.

**~11:00** — Palme arrives at **Rosenbad** (government headquarters). He **dismisses his bodyguards for the weekend**, telling them he plans to work a few more hours and stay home with his wife.

**During the day at Rosenbad:**
- Gives an interview to the magazine *Statsanställd*, saying optimistically: *"1986 is the year of possibilities. The international situation has brightened. Mistrust is receding like fog on an early spring morning."*
- Meets Iraq's ambassador **Mohammed Said al-Sahaf** (later known as "Baghdad Bob" during the 2003 Iraq War) — reportedly about Swedish weapons exports to Iran via Bofors, which angered Palme
- Meets fund managers (the government had doubled the stock turnover tax by 5.5% the day before, causing market disruption)
- Meets Norway's ambassador **Oscar Värnö** about an international peace project
- Receives a visit from a woman from the **ILO** (International Labour Organization)
- **~16:00** — Calls finance minister **Kjell-Olof Feldt**, who shares new SIFO poll numbers: Social Democrats have dropped from 45% to 42.5%

### The cinema decision

**~17:00** — **Lisbeth Palme**, at her workplace, calls their son **Mårten Palme** to discuss seeing a film at the **Grand Cinema** (biografen Grand) on Sveavägen.

**~18:10–18:30** — Olof Palme leaves Rosenbad and **walks alone** ~550 meters home to **Västerlånggatan 31 in Gamla Stan** (Old Town).

**~18:30** — Palme eats dinner with Lisbeth. He learns about the cinema plans for the first time. Makes several phone calls, including to **Bo Toresson** and **Sven Aspling**. Also speaks with Mårten by phone.

**~19:20** — Palme has a phone conversation with **Emma Rothschild** (a close friend).

**~20:00** — The decision is finalized: Olof and Lisbeth will join Mårten and his girlfriend. Mårten and his girlfriend have already bought their own tickets. By this point, Palme has dismissed his bodyguards. According to son **Joakim Palme** (stated in 2012), Olof tried to reach the bodyguard detail before leaving but couldn't get through — a claim disputed by lead investigator **Ingemar Krusell**, who said Lisbeth confirmed Palme didn't want bodyguards that night.

### The journey to the cinema

**~20:30–20:40** — The Palmes leave their apartment at Västerlånggatan 31, **unescorted**, and walk to the **Gamla Stan metro station**. Several passersby notice them and comment on the lack of bodyguards.

They take the subway from Gamla Stan to **Rådmansgatan station**, then walk to the **Grand Cinema** on Sveavägen.

**~21:00** — They meet **Mårten Palme and his girlfriend** outside the cinema. Palme has not yet bought tickets, which are nearly sold out. The ticket clerk **recognizes the Prime Minister** and sells him the **theater director's reserved seats — Row 8** (bänkrad 8), among the best in the house.

In the foyer, many cinema visitors notice Palme. A young woman approaches wanting to discuss cultural policy; Palme politely deflects. He encounters **Björn Rosengren**, chairman of TCO (confederation of professional employees). They discuss labor issues animatedly until Lisbeth shushes them as the film begins.

### The film

**Title:** *Bröderna Mozart* (*The Mozart Brothers*), directed by **Suzanne Osten**, starring **Etienne Glaser**. A Swedish comedy about an experimental theater director staging a controversial *Don Giovanni* at the Royal Opera. Premiered approximately one week earlier. Later won the **Guldbagge Award** for Best Director in 1987.

The Grand Cinema had **4 screening rooms** (Sandrew 1, 2, 3, and 4). The late showing: ads started at **21:06**, the main film at **~21:15–21:17**. The projectionist clocked the film ending at **23:03** with roughly 2 minutes of credits following — total end approximately **23:05**. Other films screening that evening: *Ran* and *Amadeus* (ending ~23:40) and *Gå och se* (ending ~23:07). **206 visitors** attended the late showing of *Bröderna Mozart*; police later identified **198** of them.

After the film, **Björn Rosengren** considers offering the Palmes a ride home but his wife tells him not to impose.

### The walk home

**~23:10–23:15** — The two couples stand outside the cinema for a brief conversation.

**~23:15** — They separate. Mårten and his girlfriend go one way. Olof and Lisbeth begin walking **south on the west side of Sveavägen**, heading toward the **Hötorget metro station**.

Near **Adolf Fredriks kyrka** (Adolf Fredrik Church), they **cross Sveavägen to the east side** — possibly because Lisbeth wanted to look at a shop window display.

They stop briefly to look at something in a shop window, then continue walking south past the **Dekorima** art supply store on the corner of Sveavägen and Tunnelgatan.

### The shooting — 23:21:30 CET

**Location:** East sidewalk of Sveavägen, at the corner with Tunnelgatan, near the Dekorima shop, in front of the **Skandia building** (Thulehuset, Sveavägen 44).

A man approached from behind the couple. He fired **two shots** from a **.357 Magnum revolver** at point-blank range (~20 cm). **First shot:** hit Palme in the back at approximately **140 cm height**, piercing the spinal cord, trachea, and aorta. Death was essentially instantaneous. **Second shot:** grazed Lisbeth Palme's back, causing a minor wound. Palme collapsed on the sidewalk, bleeding heavily from the mouth. Lisbeth knelt beside her husband, screaming for help. The killer **paused briefly**, then jogged east down Tunnelgatan.

## 2. The crime scene at Sveavägen and Tunnelgatan

### Exact location and layout

The murder occurred at the **intersection of Sveavägen and Tunnelgatan**, in the Norrmalm district of central Stockholm, on the **east sidewalk of Sveavägen**, just south of where Tunnelgatan meets Sveavägen. The spot was directly adjacent to the **Dekorima** art supply store (later Kreatima, now Urban Deli), occupying the corner of the massive Skandia building.

**Sveavägen** is one of Stockholm's major north-south arterial roads — a wide boulevard with multiple lanes of traffic and sidewalks on both sides. **Tunnelgatan** is a narrow street extending **east** from Sveavägen toward Malmskillnadsgatan. It is named after the **Brunkeberg Tunnel** (a 231-meter pedestrian tunnel under the Brunkeberg ridge, opened 1886) and features a **steep staircase of approximately 89 steps** climbing from the lower level at Sveavägen up to Malmskillnadsgatan.

### Nearby buildings and businesses

The **Skandia building (Thulehuset)** at Sveavägen 44 dominates the corner. The Skandia insurance company had its headquarters here — **Stig Engström**, later named as prime suspect, worked here as a graphic designer. Along the building's Sveavägen facade, north to south: Svensk Hemslöjd (Swedish handicrafts), a hairdresser, La Carterie, **Thulehusets tobaksaffär** (tobacconist — Nicola Fauzzi reportedly encountered the Palmes here), **Skandia's main entrance** (~60–70 meters north of the murder site), Haga Skor (shoe shop), **Götabanken** (bank, with illuminated window and ATM), and **Dekorima** at the corner with Tunnelgatan.

Other key landmarks: the **Grand Cinema** (further north on Sveavägen near Tegnérgatan), **Adolf Fredriks kyrka** (where the Palmes crossed the street), **Hötorget metro station** (their destination), and **Sabbatsberg Hospital** (~1 km away).

### The killer's escape route

After firing, the killer jogged east along **Tunnelgatan**, up the **steep 89-step staircase** to **Malmskillnadsgatan**, then along **David Bagares gata** heading east. Two witnesses (**Yvonne Nieminen** and **Ahmed Zahir**) encountered him at the top of the stairs — he was sliding in the snow, turning around repeatedly as if checking for pursuit. He was last seen by witnesses on David Bagares gata. By the time police pursued up the stairs, the killer had vanished.

### Weather and conditions

A cold, windy late-February night. **Snow-covered sidewalks** (confirmed by multiple sources; UPI reported "Palme's body in a puddle of blood on a snow-covered sidewalk"). Temperature approximately **-6°C**. Full darkness — sunset in Stockholm on February 28 is around 17:00–17:15. The intersection was **well-lit by streetlights** and illuminated shop windows (particularly the Götabanken bank window and the Dekorima display), but the late hour and weather limited visibility of facial features. The street was **moderately populated** — roughly **40 people** estimated in the vicinity, with pedestrian traffic from late cinema showings and nightlife. The shooter fired from **point-blank range** — approximately 20 cm from Palme's back.

## 3. Every known witness and what they saw

### Anders Björkman — walking 5–7 meters behind the Palmes

A critical witness who had been at a bar with friends. Wearing a **blue bubble/down jacket**. He observed the killer so close behind the Palmes that the three appeared to be "a group of three." Saw the man **place his hand on Palme's shoulder** and fire two shots. Described the killer as wearing a **dark or dark-blue knit hat with the edge rolled up** and a **dark, fluttering, coat-like garment reaching below the knees**. Described the killer's post-shooting movement as **"resilient steps"** at **"a leisurely pace"** into Tunnelgatan. After the shots, he sought refuge at the Dekorima shop doorway. Lisbeth Palme **stared directly at him**, mistaking him for the killer due to his proximity. He was employed by a Bofors company and recognized the sound of gunfire. **This is crucial**: Lisbeth Palme's later description of the killer wearing a "blue down jacket" is widely believed to actually describe Björkman, not the assassin.

### Anders Delsborn — taxi driver, first to alert police

A **Järfälla Taxi** driver, age 27, stopped at or near the intersection. Called his taxi switchboard seconds after the shooting. Also observed the Palme couple and killer as "a group of three." Said the revolver had a **relatively long barrel**. His call, relayed by switchboard operator **Ann Louise Paulsson**, reached police at **23:23:40** — the first successful alert.

### Inge Morelius — watching from his car

Sitting in his car at the intersection, waiting for friends. Saw the killer **standing on the corner of Sveavägen-Tunnelgatan, waiting for several minutes** before the shooting. Described a man in a **dark knit hat folded up at the bottom** ("like Jack Nicholson's One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest"), wearing a dark coat **stretching to just below his knees**. Saw the killer **place his left hand on Palme's shoulder** and shoot him **"calmly and professionally."** Described the killer's movements as those of **"almost an elite soldier."** Described Björkman (the man in the blue jacket) as **much shorter** than the killer.

### Anna Hage — nursing student who performed CPR

Was in a car stopped at a red light. Saw Palme fall and Lisbeth go down on her knees. **Did not hear any shots** — thought it might be a heart attack. Left the car and performed **heart compressions**, discovering blood everywhere. Described the killer as wearing **no hat**. In August 1988, she was approached by a **man in military uniform** at a café who told her she was under surveillance and that *"We have to keep the country calm."*

### Lars Jeppsson — encountered the killer on Tunnelgatan

Walking on Tunnelgatan when the shooting occurred. The killer **ran right past him**. Jeppsson was wearing a blue quilted jacket, had curly hair, and hid behind portacabins/barracks on Tunnelgatan. He attempted to chase the killer up the **89 stairs** to David Bagares gata. At the top, met Yvonne Nieminen and Ahmed Zahir. He could only **glimpse the disappearing figure** at distance. **Critical detail**: Jeppsson had seen Christer Pettersson hundreds of times — they were almost neighbors — and **stated firmly the killer was NOT Christer Pettersson.**

### Yvonne Nieminen and Ahmed Zahir — at the top of the stairs

At the top of the Tunnelgatan stairs on David Bagares gata. Nieminen described the killer as a **stocky man** in a **dark, fluttering knee-length coat of thin material**, who **turned around two or three times** as if being pursued. Said he looked **Scandinavian** and was carrying a **small clutch bag, about 4 by 6 inches**, which he seemed to be opening or closing as he ran. He was **sliding in the snow** on the pavement. She directed Jeppsson toward David Bagares gata.

### Other witnesses

- **Nicola Fauzzi**: Met the Palme couple walking on Sveavägen just before the murder; recognized them; saw a man in a blue jacket (Björkman) a few yards behind
- **Leif Ljungqvist**: In a car at a red light; made the **very first emergency call at 23:22** but it was misdirected; described the killer wearing **no hat**; may have seen a person heading **southwards** (opposite direction from main escape route), possibly indicating a **second person**
- **Jan Nilsson**: Described killer wearing a **leather jacket**
- **Jan Andersson**: Described killer wearing **no hat**
- **Egon Enoksson**: Had the impression the killer was **"quite young"**
- **Stefan Glantz**: At the scene, helped try to save Palme's life

### Lisbeth Palme's testimony

Walking slightly ahead of Olof at the moment of the shooting. Heard what she thought were **"Easter crackers"** (firecrackers). Screamed: **"What are you doing?"** Went down on her knees beside her husband. Looked around for help and saw a man 5–10 meters behind her, **staring at her**, wearing a **blue down jacket** — almost certainly Anders Björkman. At the hospital, told Chief Inspector **Åke Rimborn** she had seen **two men** at the scene. At the December 14, 1988 police lineup, she identified **Christer Pettersson**, saying: *"That's him"* and *"one can easily see who of them is an alcoholic"* — it emerged a prosecutor had told her beforehand the suspect was an alcoholic. She maintained until her death in **2018** that Pettersson was the shooter.

### Major witness contradictions

| Feature | Descriptions |
|---------|-------------|
| **Coat** | Dark/black knee-length fluttering coat (Björkman, Morelius, Nieminen) vs. blue quilted jacket (Lisbeth) vs. leather jacket (Nilsson) |
| **Hat** | Dark knit cap rolled up (Björkman, Morelius) vs. no hat (Hage, Ljungqvist, Andersson) |
| **Movement** | Initially "smooth, efficient, powerful" — after Pettersson's arrest, witnesses began describing the killer as "walking with a limp," suggesting post-arrest contamination |
| **Age** | 30–50 (most said 30–45) |
| **Height** | ~180–185 cm |

### The walkie-talkie mystery

Approximately **30 witnesses** reported men with **walkie-talkies** along the Palmes' route — from their home in Gamla Stan to the cinema and back. In 1986, walkie-talkies were police equipment (mobile phones didn't exist). At least one spoke **German/Dutch** (possibly South African Afrikaans). A SÄPO report from March–April 1986 initially concluded: *"The analysis supports that Olof Palme was under surveillance from the time he left his home until he was murdered."* Police later reversed this position.

## 4. The weapon: a .357 Magnum that was never found

**Caliber:** .357 Magnum. **Ammunition:** Winchester-Western .357 Magnum, **158 grain, metal-piercing** — designed to penetrate bulletproof vests. **Two shots** fired at point-blank range from a revolver (no shell casings ejected). The barrel was estimated at minimum **10 cm (4 inches)**, meaning a **conspicuously large handgun** — most likely a **Smith & Wesson .357 Magnum** revolver, possibly the **Model 28 "Highway Patrolman."**

**Bullet recovery** — Both bullets passed through the victims and were found on the street, not in the bodies:
- **Bullet 1** (the "Palme bullet"): Found by witness **Elisabeth Belich** near a pillar at the Tunnelgatan subway entrance at lunchtime on **March 1** — the area had been swept for evidence earlier
- **Bullet 2** (the "Lisbeth bullet"): Found by **Alfred Tavares**, a freelance Indian journalist, on the sidewalk outside **Sveavägen 29** at **6:30 AM on March 1**, in the snow about **40 meters south** of the murder scene. Tavares claimed to have analyzed the bullet's trajectory "in a self-hypnotic trance." The chain of evidence is questionable — the bullet was found **outside** the cordoned area

Both bullets matched lead fragments found in the victims' clothing. Lead isotope analysis confirmed both hit the Palmes. Expert concerns: the bullets were **surprisingly intact** for having passed through bodies and struck hard surfaces, with **no traces of blood or tissue** — unusual.

**The weapon was never found.** During the investigation, **738 weapons** were tested and approximately **500 Magnum revolvers** were test-fired. Key weapons investigated:
- **The Sucksdorff revolver**: Stolen from filmmaker **Arne Sucksdorff's** Stockholm home in 1977; the thief was a friend of drug dealer **Sigvard "Sigge" Cedergren**, who on his deathbed claimed he lent a gun to **Christer Pettersson** two months before the murder. Never recovered.
- **The Mockfjärd gun**: A Smith & Wesson Model 28 stolen in **Haparanda in 1983** with **91 metal-piercing bullets**. Used in a post office robbery in Mockfjärd, Dalarna. Lead isotope analysis confirmed the Mockfjärd robbery bullet had the **same isotopic composition** as the assassination bullets. Retrieved from a **lake in Dalarna in autumn 2006** — but was **too rusty** to determine if it fired the assassination bullets.
- **Willy Glaser's revolver**: A S&W .357 Magnum belonging to Stig Engström's friend, a firearms collector. Forensic experts **could not exclude it** as the murder weapon but could not confirm it either.
- **"GH" suspect's weapon**: The only registered .357 in the Stockholm region never submitted for testing. Owner claimed he sold it to an unknown buyer. He later shot himself when police arrived at his home in 2008.

## 5. The investigation: 34 years and seven lead investigators

### Initial chaos and critical mistakes

The first hours were catastrophic. **No roadblocks** were established. The crime scene was **not properly cordoned off** — flowers were laid by citizens, contaminating evidence. Key witnesses were **allowed to leave** before giving statements. The first emergency call at 23:22:20 was **misdirected**. **Tommy Lindström**, head of the National CID, was awakened and informed — and **went back to sleep**. Police Commissioner **Hans Holmér** was in northern Sweden, heading to Sälen for the Vasaloppet ski race. Two senior officers who eventually came in had **no basic police training** in emergencies. The alarm center's priority mechanism was **turned off** that night. A general police alert wasn't issued until **02:05** — over two hours after the shooting.

### Lead investigators (chronological)

1. **Gösta Welander** — night of the murder only
2. **Hans Holmér** — 1986–February 1987
3. **Ulf Karlsson** — 1987–1988
4. **Hans Ölvebro** — 1988–1997 (oversaw Pettersson trial)
5. **Stig Edqvist** — 1997–2012 (longest serving)
6. **Hans Melander** — 2012–2013 and 2016–2020
7. **Dag Andersson** — 2013–2016

### Chief prosecutors

1. **K.G. Svensson** — spring 1986
2. **Claes Zeime** — 1986–1987
3. **Axel Morath** — 1987–1994
4. **Solveig Riberdahl** — 1994–1996
5. **Jan Danielsson** — 1996–2000
6. **Agneta Blidberg** — 2000–2009
7. **Kerstin Skarp** — 2009–2017
8. **Krister Petersson** — 2017–2020 (closed the case)

### Hans Holmér's tenure and the PKK disaster

Holmér (1930–2002) was no ordinary police chief. He had served as **head of Sweden's security police (SÄPO) from 1970–1976** at a remarkably young age, and was closely tied to the Social Democratic Party. On March 1, 1986, he returned from northern Sweden, held his first press conference at noon, and **appointed himself as lead investigator** — without formal designation. He became the public face of the investigation, holding daily press conferences. At one early briefing, he **brandished a Smith & Wesson .357 Magnum**, effectively locking the investigation onto that specific model for years.

Within one week, Holmér fixated on the **PKK (Kurdistan Workers' Party)**. On **January 20, 1987**, approximately 200 officers rounded up **22 Kurds** ("Operation Alpha") — with zero evidence. All were released. This spectacular failure led to his forced resignation in **February 1987**.

### The Ebbe Carlsson affair (1988)

After Holmér's removal, publisher **Ebbe Carlsson** (1947–1992) — a friend of Holmér's — privately continued the PKK investigation with backing from **National Police Commissioner Nils Erik Åhmansson** and **Justice Minister Anna-Greta Leijon**, who wrote letters of recommendation. Carlsson obtained illegal wiretapping equipment, access to secret documents, and an undercover police car. The scheme was exposed by Expressen reporter **Per Wendel** on June 1, 1988. Leijon, Åhmansson, and the SÄPO chief were all forced to resign. Carlsson was fined and died of AIDS on August 3, 1992.

### Scale of the investigation

- **250+ shelf meters** of investigation material
- **700,000 pages** of documents
- **10,000+ people** interviewed
- **90,000 people** included in the preliminary investigation; **40,000 named**
- **22,430 different points of interest**
- **134 people** confessed to the murder (29 directly to police)
- **738 weapons** tested; ~500 Magnum revolvers test-fired
- **4,000+ vehicles** investigated
- **Cost: SEK 600 million** (~$71 million)
- **Reward: SEK 50 million** (~$7 million) — unclaimed
- Sweden's statute of limitations on murder was **changed in 2010** specifically to prevent this case from expiring

## 6. The suspects: eight trails and no conviction

### Christer Pettersson — convicted, then acquitted

**Background:** Born April 23, 1947. Attended theatrical school but suffered a head injury, leading to chronic alcoholism and drug addiction with **63 prior convictions**. In **1970**, he stabbed a man to death during a street brawl just around the corner from the future murder scene — was sentenced to psychiatric care, released in under two years.

**How he became a suspect:** In 1988, investigators picked up rumors from the drug world. Key informant: drug dealer **Sigvard "Sigge" Cedergren**, who placed Pettersson outside the Grand Cinema that night. But SVT's *Uppdrag Granskning* later revealed that police officer **Thure Nässén** had interrogated Cedergren **43 times**, and three homicide officers alleged Nässén systematically fed Cedergren information. Phone surveillance showed Cedergren was **at home on the phone** during the murder. Cedergren changed his story repeatedly — first naming thief **Lars-Inge Svartenbrandt**, then a lineup officer, then singer **Ted Gärdestad**, and finally Pettersson.

**Arrested:** December 14, 1988. **Lineup:** Lisbeth Palme identified Pettersson from a 10-person lineup, but with **"extremely gross errors"** (the appeals court's language): she was told beforehand the suspect was an alcoholic, and Pettersson's gaunt, unshaven appearance contrasted sharply with the healthier-looking fillers.

**Trial:** Stockholm District Court, starting June 5, 1989. Six lay judges voted to convict; two professional judges voted to acquit. Convicted by majority on **July 27, 1989**, sentenced to life. **Acquittal:** Unanimously acquitted by the **Svea Court of Appeal** later in 1989 — no weapon found, unreliable identification, no motive. In 1998, the Supreme Court refused a retrial. Pettersson died **September 29, 2004**, at age 57, from a cerebral hemorrhage following a head injury.

### Victor Gunnarsson and the LaRouche movement

Born March 31, 1953. A right-wing extremist connected to the **European Workers' Party (EAP)**, the Swedish branch of the Lyndon LaRouche movement — which called Palme "a Soviet asset." Anti-Palme literature was found in his apartment. Forensic particles on his jacket suggested he had **recently fired a gun**. Arrested March 17, 1986; released March 20 after eyewitness identifications proved unreliable. Later emigrated to the US, where his body was found on **January 7, 1994**, in Deep Gap, North Carolina — shot twice in the head with a .22 caliber pistol. Former police officer **Lamont C. Underwood** was convicted (love triangle).

### The PKK / Kurdish trail

Hans Holmér's obsession. The Palme government had classified the PKK as a terrorist organization after several ex-PKK members were murdered in Sweden in 1984–85. **Operation Alpha** (January 20, 1987) rounded up 22 Kurds with no evidence. All released. The 1999 government review commission concluded Holmér's pursuit amounted to **"deliberate misdirection."** In 2001, PKK leader **Abdullah Öcalan** was interviewed in Turkish prison and alleged a dissident Kurdish group might have been responsible — it led nowhere. The weakest major lead.

### The South Africa trail

One week before his murder, Palme gave the keynote at the **Swedish People's Parliament Against Apartheid** (February 21, 1986), declaring: *"Apartheid cannot be reformed, it has to be abolished."* A SADF Military Intelligence document dated **October 15, 1985** concluded Palme *"should be seen as an enemy of the State."*

Key figures: **Craig Williamson** — former South African "super spy" who infiltrated anti-apartheid organizations and ran **"Operation Long Reach"**; admitted to ordering the 1982 letter-bomb murder of Ruth First. **Eugene de Kock** ("Prime Evil") — at his 1996 trial, testified that Operation Long Reach **"played a role"** in Palme's murder. **Anthony White** — former Rhodesian Selous Scout named by hit-squad member **Dirk Coetzee** as the actual shooter. **Bertil Wedin** — Swedish mercenary in Cyprus, recruited by Williamson; admitted in a British court to working for South African intelligence.

Swedish police visited South Africa in October 1996 but found insufficient evidence. Tommy Lindström, head of the CID, publicly stated in **2010** that South Africa was his **#1 suspect**. Stieg Larsson spent ~10 years investigating this trail. Considered one of the most compelling leads given South Africa's documented history of extraterritorial assassinations.

### The police trail ("polispåret")

The **"Baseball Gang" (Basebolligan)** — police officers from the Norrmalm district known for violence, right-wing extremism, and Nazi sympathies. Key officers identified in the 1999 review commission (SOU 1999:88):

- **Carl Gustav Östling**: Documented Nazi, weapons dealer, possessed the same unusual ammunition as the murder weapon. Absented himself from hospital on the day of the murder. A taxi driver reportedly picked him up near the murder scene 4–5 minutes after the shooting.
- **Stellan Åkerbring**: Organized secretive right-wing meetings with Nazi march music. Day after the murder, reportedly **"toasted with champagne for the murder."**
- **Thomas Piltz**: Multiple witnesses saw him near the murder scene speaking into a walkie-talkie, and at a bus on the extension of the killer's escape route.
- **Claes Djurfeldt**: Right-wing extremist with a revolver and ammunition matching the murder weapon at home. Was in a riot police van positioned **above Tunnelgatan** and had left the vehicle alone, standing on the ridge when shots were fired.
- **Anti Avsan**: Union representative described as *"a leader type and one of the worst Nazis."* Two Finnish women reported seeing a man at the Dekorima corner speaking into a **walkie-talkie in Finnish** with a **revolver in his other hand** just before the murder. Avsan later served as a **member of the Swedish parliament** for the Moderate Party.

The 1999 commission stated it *"cannot be excluded that individual police officers were involved"* and criticized the investigation for never properly pursuing this trail.

### Stay Behind / NATO / Gladio

Sweden had a **stay-behind network** (part of NATO's covert Cold War infrastructure), confirmed by General **Bengt Gustafsson** in 1990. It was led by **Alvar Lindencrona**, director of the **Thule Insurance Company**, with offices in **Thulehuset on Sveavägen** — the very building at the murder scene. Members had far-right sympathies and access to weapons. The overlap with the police trail and walkie-talkie observations has kept this theory alive, but no direct evidence links Stay Behind to the murder. Classified records make definitive research impossible — SÄPO reportedly **"lost"** its file on Palme, opened in the 1950s.

### The Bofors arms deal

In early 1986, Sweden was finalizing a **$1.4 billion contract** for Bofors to supply 410 howitzers to India — signed March 24, 1986, barely a month after the murder. Later revealed to involve **$9 million+ in kickbacks**. Historian **Jan Bondeson** theorized Palme may have discovered the corruption the day of the murder. Suspicious related deaths included **Carl Algernon**, State Weapons Inspector, who fell in front of a subway train in January 1987 — 30 minutes after meeting the head of Nobel Industries (a Bofors co-owner).

## 7. Stig Engström: named in 2020, cleared in 2025

### Who he was

**Born:** February 26, 1934, in Bombay, British India, to affluent Swedish parents. His father worked for industrialist **Ivar Kreuger**. Grew up in colonial India with domestic staff. Sent back to Sweden at age 12. Trained as a graphic designer; worked from the late 1960s at the **Skandia Insurance Company** in their headquarters at Sveavägen — **approximately 60 meters from the murder site**. Briefly associated with the **Moderate Party** (conservatives) in Täby. Described by acquaintances as having **"a deep anti-Palme sentiment."** Completed military service (reaching sergeant rank), was a **shooting club member**, and worked for the Swedish military illustrating field manuals. Known as attention-seeking and dramatic. A childhood friend told Expressen: *"He was not a mythomaniac, but it was along those lines."*

**Height:** 182 cm. **Age at time of murder:** 52 (turned 52 two days before). He had growing **alcohol and financial problems**. His close friend **Willy Glaser** was a firearms collector who owned a Smith & Wesson .357 Magnum revolver.

### His presence at the crime scene

According to Skandia's time clock, Engström clocked out at **23:20** (clock was 1 minute fast — actual time **23:19**), just **two minutes** before the shooting. He chatted with security guard **Henry Olofsson** for what Olofsson recalled was "a couple of minutes." He claimed he went to the crime scene, was one of the first to arrive, helped reposition Palme's body, spoke with Lisbeth Palme, pointed out the killer's escape route, and tried to chase police to pass on information.

**No other witness corroborated any of this.** Head of Investigation Hans Melander stated in 2020: *"No one saw anyone resembling Engström in the role he described himself playing."* Over **four police interrogations** and several media interviews, his story changed significantly. He could identify people at the scene whom the **killer** would have encountered (like Jeppsson behind the portacabins on Tunnelgatan) but not those who arrived after the shooting. He provided false information about his movements.

On **April 6, 1986**, when police failed to invite him to their crime reconstruction, he contacted a TV journalist and **staged his own reconstruction** in the clothes he wore that night — broadcast on SVT's *Rapport*. In a **1992 interview**, he told journalist Jan Arvidsson: *"I personally would have used a more versatile weapon, a smaller caliber... If I had been the murderer."*

### The 2020 conclusion

On **June 10, 2020**, at a digital press conference during COVID-19, Chief Prosecutor **Krister Petersson** identified Engström as the probable perpetrator and formally closed the case, since the suspect was deceased (Engström died — apparently by suicide — on **June 26, 2000**, found with an empty whiskey bottle and painkillers). Petersson stated: *"There is one suspect that we can't get around: Stig Engström."* He said that had the current investigation group been in charge in 1986, *"Engström would have been remanded in custody."* He acknowledged the evidence was **purely circumstantial** and insufficient for conviction at trial.

The evidence presented: proximity to the crime scene, clothing matching witness descriptions, unreliable and changing testimony, weapons background, anti-Palme sentiment, and behavioral pattern of inserting himself into the investigation. **Not presented:** no forensic evidence, no murder weapon, no eyewitness identification of Engström as the shooter, no clear motive.

### The 2025 reversal

On **December 18, 2025**, Director of Public Prosecutions **Lennart Guné** reversed the designation: Engström is **no longer** considered the suspect. Guné cited Lisbeth Palme's identification of Pettersson as incompatible with Engström, witness statements that the killer **stood waiting** for the couple (incompatible with Engström's spontaneous encounter), no evidence of how Engström had the weapon, and Engström's detailed early statements being *"difficult to explain other than that they were self-experienced"* — suggesting he was a genuine witness. Guné changed the closure reason from "suspect deceased" to *"it is not possible to prove who the perpetrator is."*

A **mock trial at Lund University** on **May 5, 2025** (organized by Professor **Christian Dahlmann**, with five international judges) **unanimously acquitted** Engström, citing absence of forensic links and unresolved timeline gaps.

## 8. Key people in the case

**Lisbeth Palme** (née Beck-Friis, 1931–2018): Child psychologist, chairwoman of Swedish UNICEF Committee 1986–1999. The most important witness — and the most controversial. Her identification of Pettersson was undermined by lineup errors and confusion between the killer and witness Björkman. Maintained Pettersson's guilt until death.

**Hans Holmér** (1930–2002): SÄPO chief 1970–76, Stockholm police commissioner, self-appointed lead investigator. Pursued the PKK trail obsessively. Wrote *Olof Palme är skjuten!* blaming prosecutors for the investigation's failure. Died in Skåne.

**Stieg Larsson** (1954–2004): At the TT News Agency on the murder night, sent to map the crime scene. Spent ~10 years investigating the murder, focused on South African and far-right connections. His research won the **Guldspaden (journalism prize) in 1987** (under anonymity). Founded *Expo* magazine in 1995. Left ~15 boxes of research material, discovered in 2013 by **Jan Stocklassa**, who expanded the investigation into *The Man Who Played with Fire* (2018), adapted into a 2023 HBO documentary.

**Leif GW Persson** (b. 1945): Sweden's foremost criminologist, professor at the National Police Board 1992–2012. Called the investigation *"the largest in global police history."* Rejected both Pettersson and Engström as suspects. His *"Story of a Crime" trilogy* (2002–2007) is the definitive fictional account of events surrounding the assassination. Called the 2020 Engström conclusion *"a colossal disappointment."*

**Thomas Pettersson**: Freelance journalist who spent 12 years investigating, won the **Guldspaden in 2018** for his *Filter* magazine articles building the case against Engström. His book *Den osannolika mördaren* (The Unlikely Murderer, 2018) was adapted into a **Netflix series** (November 5, 2021, 5 episodes, starring **Robert Gustafsson** as Engström — Gustafsson was actually at the same cinema screening as Palme that night).

## 9. Forensic evidence: almost nothing

The forensic record is remarkable for its emptiness. The **only physical evidence** left by the assassin: **two metal-piercing .357 Magnum bullets** — found by civilians, not police. **No fingerprints** linked to the killer. **No DNA evidence**. **No shell casings** (revolvers don't eject them). **No clothing fibers** definitively connected. **No items** belonging to the killer found at the scene. No blood or tissue traces on the recovered bullets (unusual). The crime scene was **not properly sealed** — contaminated by well-wishers laying flowers, pedestrians walking through, and delayed formal investigation (which didn't begin until **10:00 the next morning**, almost 11 hours after the murder).

The composite police sketch — **"The Phantom" (fantombilden)** — was released on **March 6, 1986**, based on a female cartoonist/artist who encountered a mysterious man near the scene. She later **disowned the image**, and it was determined she probably had not seen the actual killer. The sketch generated **7,000–8,000 tips** and massive false leads but is now considered worthless.

## 10. Why Palme had no bodyguard and why he was hated

### The unguarded Prime Minister

Palme dismissed his SÄPO bodyguards at Rosenbad around 11:00, telling them he'd stay home that evening. The cinema decision was made **hours later**, around 18:30–20:00. Son Joakim claims Palme tried to reach the detail before leaving but couldn't; investigators dispute this. It was part of **Swedish political culture** that leaders lived ordinary lives — a culture that persisted even after the murder, until Foreign Minister **Anna Lindh was stabbed to death** without bodyguards in a Stockholm department store in **2003**. SÄPO was also running a secret operation in Stockholm that night, codenamed **"Così fan tutte"** (after the Mozart opera), whose purpose has never been revealed. When investigators sought SÄPO's file on Palme — opened in the 1950s — SÄPO reported it had **"disappeared."**

### Palmehat: the phenomenon of Palme hatred

Palme was the most polarizing figure in modern Swedish politics. He marched with North Vietnam's ambassador in 1968. He compared US bombing to Treblinka in 1972, causing the US to **recall its ambassador**. He was the first Western leader to visit Castro's Cuba. He gave $100,000 to Allende's daughter. He championed the ANC, the PLO, and nuclear disarmament while condemning both superpowers. Domestically, his employee investment funds alarmed business circles. Right-wing magazine *Contra* distributed **dartboards with Palme's face** and ran an issue with **Palme's face on the cover with a target** two years before his death. He was called a Soviet agent, a class traitor, and was reportedly the subject of KGB-controlled telepathy theories from a marine officer. Friend Harry Schein noted after the murder: *"But where did all the hate pictures of Olof Palme go, with the diabolic expression, the ugly contorted faces?"*

## 11. A nation's loss of innocence

The assassination was the **first murder of a Swedish national leader since King Gustav III in 1792**. Cuba, Portugal, and Nicaragua declared three days of national mourning. India, Argentina, and Yugoslavia declared a day of mourning. **125 nations** sent delegations to Palme's funeral at the **Blue Hall of Stockholm City Hall** on March 15, 1986. He is buried in the cemetery of **Adolf Fredriks kyrka**.

A **golden/bronze plaque** is embedded in the sidewalk at the exact spot. The inscription reads: *"PÅ DENNA PLATS MORDADES SVERIGES STATSMINISTER OLOF PALME DEN 28 FEBRUARI 1986."* Half of Tunnelgatan was renamed **Olof Palmes gata**. The **Olof Palme Memorial Fund** awards an annual **$100,000 prize** (recipients include Nelson Mandela, Human Rights Watch, and Bellingcat). The current chair is former PM **Stefan Löfven**. Streets bearing Palme's name exist across the world — in Athens, Thessaloniki, Berlin, London, and dozens of other cities.

The unsolved case profoundly shaped Swedish culture. The assassination is universally described as Sweden's **"loss of innocence"** — a nation that hadn't fought a war since 1814 where the PM rode the subway without guards. The botched investigation eroded public trust in institutions. The trauma fueled the rise of **Nordic noir crime fiction**: Stieg Larsson was literally at the crime scene that night, and his *Millennium Trilogy* — with its themes of institutional corruption, cover-ups, and right-wing extremism — sold over **100 million copies**. **Henning Mankell's Wallander series**, deeply colored by the post-Palme atmosphere, began in 1991. The genre's core premise — a perfect welfare state concealing dark secrets — traces directly to 1986.

On **February 28, 2026** — the 40th anniversary — journalists **Lars Borgnäs** and **Per Janse** presented **~1,000 signatures** calling for a **sanningskommission** (truth commission) with legal power to cut through official secrecy. The case remains closed. The murder weapon was never found. The killer was never conclusively identified. Sweden's longest and most expensive criminal investigation ended not with an answer, but with the formal admission that the truth may never be known.
